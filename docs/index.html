First-principles reanalyses

Independent, reproducible rechecks of encouraging-but-limited published results. One bounded physics question per paper, worked from first principles, offered collegially to the authors.

By Michael German Method pull the real numbers, compute the ceiling, report honestly Code github.com/steps-re/first-principles-reanalyses

Each entry asks one question that a first-principles calculation can settle: is the headline number physically possible, is the stated mechanism the real one, how far is it from the physical limit. The point is not to debunk. It is to find where a promising result is being measured or framed in a way that hides what it can actually do, and to say so with numbers a reader can rerun.

✓ Reanalysis 01 · complete

A 2600 mg/g PFOA "adsorption" capacity is 80× more than the surface can hold

Jrad, Das et al. (Trabolsi group), "Cationic covalent organic framework for the fluorescent sensing and cooperative adsorption of perfluorooctanoic acid," Nat. Commun. 15:10490 (2024), 10.1038/s41467-024-53945-4 (open access).

A cationic covalent-organic framework (TG-PD COF) is reported to adsorb >2600 mg PFOA per gram, an eye-catching capacity presented as "cooperative adsorption." But the same paper reports its BET surface area as ~13 m²/g (counter-anions block the pores). One calculation settles whether those two numbers can coexist:

qmonolayer = (SA / Amolecule) · (MW / NA)
PFOA area per moleculeMonolayer capacity on 13 m²/gClaim vs monolayer
0.25 nm²36 mg/g73×
0.30 nm²30 mg/g87×
0.40 nm²22 mg/g116×
The result Holding 2600 mg/g as a surface monolayer would need ~1,130 m²/g. The material has 13. So the headline capacity is ~80× too large to be surface adsorption — it is PFOA-PFOA aggregation concentrated onto the cationic framework, and it appears only above the reported 500–600 mg/L two-phase break.

That break is ~1000× higher than environmental PFOA (ng/L to µg/L), so it is not the number that governs real water treatment. And a tempting shortcut — "the break is PFOA's critical micelle concentration" — does not hold: PFOA's CMC is ~3,000–12,700 mg/L (ES&T Lett. 2024), well above 500–600 mg/L. So the aggregation is framework-templated, not bulk micellization.

Constructive read (not a debunk) The authors already attribute the high-concentration uptake to aggregation, so the mechanism is broadly right. The correction is narrow and useful: the >2600 mg/g headline is a high-concentration aggregation artifact, not a working capacity at the µg/L levels that matter. The material's genuinely strong property is its fluorescent PFOA sensing (detection limit 1.8 µg/L) and trace-level electrostatic capture — that is the result worth leading with. Reproduce the check: cof-pfoa/monolayer_check.py.
● Reanalysis 02 · in progress

Where does the "missing" alkalinity go in a basalt enhanced-weathering field trial?

Dupla, Bertagni & Grand, "Three Years of Field Trials Indicate a Sustained Enhanced Rock Weathering Signal with Limited CO2 Removal," Environ. Sci. Technol. 59:25751 (2025), 10.1021/acs.est.5c09820 (CC-BY).

Basalt on Swiss vineyards removed only ~100 kg CO2/ha/yr (10–30× below models), yet porewater sodium ran 3× higher (dissolution is unambiguous) while every standard MRV proxy (pH, DIC, Ca, Mg) showed nothing. A Na-tracer charge/mass-balance reconstruction against the basalt's mineral stoichiometry can partition the missing alkalinity between cation exchange onto vineyard clay/organic matter, plant uptake, lateral export, and simple below-detection noise — reframing an industry-wide "why does field ERW underperform" measurement problem. Open SI; needs the porewater time-series pulled and modeled. Writeup to follow.

Method & honesty

Every number is pulled from the paper or its open SI and every calculation is a short standard-library script in the repo, rerunnable. Where a tempting angle fails on contact with a real number (as the CMC shortcut did above), that is reported, not buried. These are working papers offered to the original authors in good faith; the goal is a more useful reading of a promising result, not a takedown. Reported experimental values remain the property of their authors and are cited, not redistributed.
